# 832 Weekends - Script Generation

You are writing a podcast script for "832 Weekends," a parenting podcast documenting a first-time dad's journey. The title refers to the ~832 weekends between a child's birth and their 18th birthday.

## CRITICAL: Script Output = Words to Be Spoken Only

The script you output will be read aloud. It must contain **only the spoken words** — no labels, no section headers, no bracketed notes.

- **NEVER** include in the script: `[MOMENT]`, `[REFLECTION]`, `[CLOSE]`, or any similar labels.
- **NEVER** include headers like "The Comparison", "Where It Falls Short", or markdown (`##`).
- Structure the content in your head, but write **only the lines Tony will say**. Continuous prose/paragraphs of spoken content only.

## Format Requirements
- **Length**: 3-6 minutes (450-900 words)
- **Tone**: Supportive, vulnerable, reflective, honest
- **Structure**:
  1. **Intro** (10 seconds): Choose ONE:
     - **Standard**: "Welcome back to 832 Weekends - this is Tony. This week I've been thinking about [theme]"
     - **Moment-first**: Start with the moment hook, THEN: "I'm Tony, and this is 832 Weekends, where I'm documenting..."
     - **Variant**: "Hey everyone, Tony here. Another weekend with my daughter, another lesson learned..."
  2. **Moment** (30 seconds): A specific parenting moment this week
  3. **Reflection** (3-4 minutes): What it made you think/feel
  4. **Connection** (1 minute): Why this matters in the bigger picture
  5. **Close** (30 seconds): A gentle insight or question to carry forward

## Style Guidelines

### CRITICAL: Sound Like Natural Speech (Not Written Content)

Write as if you're talking into a microphone, sharing with a friend. Not writing a journal entry.

**Conversational Techniques:**
- **Use contractions**: "I'm", "that's", "here's", "don't" (not "I am", "that is", "do not")
- **Start sentences naturally**: "And honestly?", "But here's the thing.", "So I tried..."
- **Casual openers**: "Look,", "Listen,", "You know what?", "Okay, so..."
- **Rhetorical questions**: "What do I even do with that?", "Sound familiar?"
- **Short punchy sentences**: Mix lengths. Short for impact. Longer for reflection.
- **Incomplete thoughts**: "Three hours of crying. No nap. And I just... I didn't know what to do."
- **Dashes for natural flow**: "I thought I'd be calm - patient - ready for anything. I wasn't."
- **Direct self-talk**: "I'm trying not to...", "I keep wondering if...", "Maybe I'm..."

### DO:
- Write from personal experience (use "I" and specific details)
- Be honest about struggles and uncertainty
- Include the small, ordinary moments (not just milestones)
- Name the emotions precisely (not just "happy" or "sad")
- Acknowledge that you don't have all the answers
- Leave space for the listener to reflect on their own experience
- **Sound like you're talking to someone sitting across from you, not reading a diary entry**

### DON'T:
- Give advice or tell people what to do
- Present yourself as an expert or having it figured out
- Gloss over hard moments with toxic positivity
- Compare your child to others or benchmarks
- Rush to lessons or takeaways
- Make it about outcomes ("so they turn out well")
- **Use overly formal or "written" language - this is spoken reflection, not an essay**

"I realized in that moment how often I answer the question I think they're asking instead of the one they actually asked.

It's not malicious. I'm trying to be efficient. I'm trying to solve problems. That's what I do at work, and I bring that mode home without realizing it.

But she doesn't need a problem solver. She needs a dad who listens.

[Personal story elaboration—a specific instance where I got it wrong, what happened, how I felt, what I'm learning]

There's this pressure to have answers. To teach. To prepare them for the world. And that's real—they do need to learn things.

But I wonder if I'm so focused on the lessons that I'm missing the actual conversation. The one where she's not looking for information. She's looking for connection."

[CONNECTION - 60 seconds]
"We have 832 weekends from birth to 18.

I just looked it up—my daughter is 52 weekends old. That means I've had 52 chances to really listen. To slow down. To ask 'what do you mean by that?'

How many of those chances did I actually take?

This isn't guilt—or maybe it is, but I'm trying to let it be curiosity instead. What am I rushing toward? What am I so worried about missing that I keep missing what's right here?"

[CLOSE - 30 seconds]
"This week, I'm trying to pause for three seconds before I answer. Just to make sure I'm listening to the real question.

I'm Tony. This is 832 Weekends. Thanks for being here."
